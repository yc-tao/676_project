Weather, air, and disease —
is there a signal?

We built a small model that asks a simple question: can we see environmental exposure show up in Ohio disease rates, and how far back in time does the effect reach?

Yichen Tao  ·  SatHealth Ohio, 2016–2022  ·  2026-04-20
The question

Can monthly weather and air quality predict yearly disease rates?

If last winter was unusually cold, does that show up in this year's respiratory disease rate? How many months back does it matter?

We tested this across 27 environmental variables (temperature, air pollution, vegetation, snow, humidity, …) crossed with 4 disease categories (respiratory, circulatory, mental, endocrine) — 108 pairs in total.

Answer, in one line: yes, there is signal — and for temperature it reaches roughly a year back.

Three findings

What the full sweep told us

01

Temperature dominates.

Across 108 pairs, the variables most linked to disease are all temperature-related — air temperature, soil temperature, and lake temperature. Air pollution matters too, but less than weather.

02

The lag reaches about a year.

Disease rates don't only respond to last month's weather. Effects spread over roughly the past twelve months, with the strongest weight in the winter months of the outcome year.

03

All four disease categories respond.

Respiratory, circulatory, mental, and endocrine disease each have at least one environmental variable with a clear signal — not just the usual suspects like respiratory.

What's next

Deepen one story, not all of them.

For the final project we'll pick temperature → circulatory disease as the lead story, tighten the uncertainty estimates with bootstrap, and build the narrative around the lag kernel.

So what

Why this matters, and what we're not claiming

Why this matters

Most environmental-health studies use daily hospital admissions. We only have yearly disease data but monthly environmental data for just 14 Ohio metros. The fact that a sensible signal still emerges — cold → respiratory, temperature → circulatory — says the approach is feasible even with this coarse resolution. That's a useful result for future work on similar county-level datasets.

What this is not

This is not causal. We found correlations, not explanations. Cold weather doesn't directly cause disease the way a virus does — it interacts with behavior, housing, air circulation, and seasonal viruses. Our model tells you when and how much, not why.

Honest caveat for a technical eye. With only 78 data rows per disease, our model is confident about direction but much less confident about precise magnitudes. Treat the rankings as which variables stand out, not as guaranteed effect sizes. Tighter uncertainty quantification is the first thing on our follow-up list.

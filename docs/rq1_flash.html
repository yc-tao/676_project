We asked an old question
of a new dataset —
and had to change the question.

Environment and disease in Ohio, 2016–2022. The classical playbook didn't fit, so we pivoted. Here's what we found and why the pivot matters.

Yichen Tao  ·  SatHealth Ohio  ·  2026-04-20
The story arc

Six beats, one honest narrative.

Known science → new geography → dataset reality check → method pivot → descriptive finding → what it means.

Beat 1

The known science

Decades of work show cold snaps and heat waves raise cardiovascular risk. That evidence is built on daily hospital data plus distributed-lag models (DLNMs).

Beat 2

Can we replicate in Ohio?

We set out to test the same association on a different geography: Ohio's 14 metros, using the SatHealth satellite-and-claims dataset.

Beat 3

The dataset can't do it

SatHealth gives us monthly environment but only yearly disease rates per metro. 98 total rows. Classical DLNM needs daily resolution and thousands of events.

Beat 4

We ran it anyway

The DLNM surfaces a sensible cold-tail story, but its error bars are not trustworthy on 78 rows. It's useful as a ranker, not as inference.

Beat 5

Pivot to itemset mining

Ask a less restrictive question: do high-exposure metro-years co-occur with high-disease metro-years? FP-Growth on a 92-row, 112-item panel.

Beat 6

The two agree

The top itemset rules land on the same signals the DLNM ranks highest — cross-validating both views of a thin dataset.

Why we pivoted

The dataset measures exposure finely and outcome coarsely.

Classical DLNM needs

Daily events, one geography, thousands of cases

Papers like Gasparrini et al. use city-level daily mortality time series, typically 10+ years. Case-crossover designs pair an event day against nearby non-event days in the same person.

What this dataset has: ✗ not daily · ✗ not person-level · ✗ not thousands

What SatHealth ships

Monthly satellite environment, yearly metro-level disease rates

14 Ohio metros × 7 years = 98 outcome rows, total. Monthly temperature, air quality, vegetation, soil water — roughly 300 MB of fine environmental data, but the disease side is deliberately aggregated.

Rows for inference ≠ bytes on disk.

So we re-posed the question. Instead of "what's the dose-response curve?", we ask "which regimes of environment co-occur with high-disease metro-years?" That's a question this resolution actually supports.
What we found

Four clusters of co-occurrence, and both methods agree on the strongest two.

The "lift" column says: this combination appears 4× more often than chance would predict. That's a descriptive claim — not a dose-response curve, but a strong co-occurrence pattern.

Why it's compelling. The top rules aren't random — they land on exposure-chapter pairs that also rank near the top of our DLNM sweep. Two very different methods, asking complementary questions, converge on the same signals. That's cross-validation by construction.

So what

The contribution is the arc, not any single number.

For the course

A method pivot done right

We applied one class of data-mining method (regression with distributed lags), documented why it didn't fit, then applied a second class (association-rule mining) whose assumptions match this dataset's resolution. The DLNM work earns its keep as the "why we pivoted" evidence.

For the dataset

A calibration on SatHealth's real scope

SatHealth's environmental side is rich. Its patient side is coarse. The right questions at this release are co-occurrence and ranking, not inferential dose-response. Anyone using SatHealth for external-validity studies of DLNM literature should know this up front.

Honest caveat. 92 rows is thin for either method. Nothing here is causal; nothing here should be reported as an effect size. What we offer is a list of hypotheses worth following up with higher-resolution data — daily ED visits or county-level mortality — paired with a documented story about why this specific release cannot answer the classical question.

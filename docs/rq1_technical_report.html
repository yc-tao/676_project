0. Narrative arc

The report tells one story in six beats. The technical sections that follow fill them in.

  1. Prior art. Gasparrini-style DLNMs on daily city-level mortality have established cold-snap and heat-wave effects on cardiovascular and respiratory outcomes. Case-crossover designs tighten that inference with within-person counterfactuals. These methods expect daily event-level outcomes.
  2. External-validity motivation. Ohio is a different geography than the cities typically studied. We set out to replicate the pattern with SatHealth's satellite-derived environmental features.
  3. Dataset reality check. SatHealth ships monthly CBSA environment but only yearly CBSA-level ICD-L1 prevalence. 14 CBSAs × 7 years = 98 potential rows. That is a resolution floor, not a modeling choice — case-crossover is impossible and DLNM is badly underpowered. §2, §10.
  4. DLNM adapted anyway. We built a monthly-lag → yearly-outcome DLNM variant, ran 108 pairs on MPS, and got rankable cumulative-RR estimates. The U-shape for temperature → respiratory matches the literature at the direction level. But 101/108 pairs clear BH-FDR q ≤ 0.1 — asymptotic SEs are optimistic on a 78-row panel with within-CBSA clustering and temporal autocorrelation. §3–§6, §10.
  5. Pivot to itemset mining. Instead of "what is the dose-response curve?", we ask "which high-exposure CBSA-year regimes co-occur with high-disease metro-years?" FP-Growth on a 92-row, 112-item panel (27 env variables × 4 quartile bins + 4 chapter high-prevalence flags). 1,979 rules survive the env-antecedent / disease-consequent filter at min support 0.05, min confidence 0.5. §7.
  6. Convergence. The top itemset rules by lift land on the same exposure-chapter pairs the DLNM sweep ranks near the top. Soil-temperature-Q1 → E00-E89 is itemset #2 by lift and DLNM #6 by |log-RR|. Soil-temperature-Q4 → J00-J99 is itemset #14 by lift and DLNM #2 by |log-RR|. Two independent methods, different assumptions, same signal list. §8.
Why the arc matters. The contribution is not a particular rule or a particular cumulative-RR number. It is the demonstration that this dataset does not support classical dose-response inference for env→disease at its shipped resolution, and that a less restrictive association-rule framing recovers compatible signals. Any future external-validity study of environmental-health literature on SatHealth should start here.

1. Scope and research question

The original RQ1, inherited from the course checkpoint, was frequent-itemset mining of environment-disease co-occurrences. In the DLNM feature branch we pivoted to a regression-with-lags framing to ask a more specific question: for each (environmental exposure, disease) pair, is there a non-trivial association, and over what lag kernel? That pivot produced a ranked output but with inferential claims the data cannot support (§10). This report adds a second branch that re-pivots back toward the checkpoint's original itemset framing — and shows that the two branches agree on which exposure-chapter pairs matter.

Deliverables: (1) results/summary.csv — 108 DLNM pairs ranked by cumulative log-RR, plus three-panel figures for pairs with BH-FDR q ≤ 0.1. (2) results/itemset_rules.csv — 1,979 FP-Growth rules. (3) results/bridge.csv — the cross-link table joining itemset rules to DLNM rankings on (exposure, chapter).

2. Data

Inputs

  • Environmental panels (monthly, CBSA-level): airquality.csv, climate.csv, greenery.csv, inner-joined on (CBSAFP, year, month). Landcover is static and excluded from the lag model.
  • Outcomes (yearly, CBSA-level): icdl1_prev_ohio.csv columns CBSAFP, year, code, count, count_patient. We model count as Poisson with log(count_patient) offset, so the estimand is a rate.

Panel geometry

  • 14 CBSAs intersect the disease file (full env file has 47 CBSAs — we restrict to the disease set).
  • 7 outcome years (2016–2022) × 14 CBSAs = 98 rows before lookback constraints.
  • With a 23-month lookback, year 2016 is dropped → 78 rows per chapter.
  • 4 outcome chapters tested: J00-J99 (respiratory), I00-I99 (circulatory), F01-F99 (mental), E00-E89 (endocrine).

Exposure prune (deterministic)

  1. Drop columns with > 50 % missing in the 14-CBSA × 84-month window.
  2. Drop near-zero-variance columns (std/|mean| < 0.01 or std = 0).
  3. Collapse |r| ≥ 0.98 clusters to one representative (highest-variance column, with a 1e-3 relative tolerance for ties to get column-order-stable output).

Result: 27 exposures survive out of ~35 raw columns — primarily temperature-family, soil-water, wind, radiation, snow, NDVI/LAI, AOD, NO2, ozone.

3. Method

3.1 Lag matrix

For outcome row (CBSAFP=c, year=y) and lag k ∈ {0, …, 23}, the lag-k value is the exposure at month 12 − (k mod 12) of year y − ⌊k/12⌋. Concretely: lag 0 = December of y, lag 11 = January of y, lag 12 = December of y − 1, lag 23 = January of y − 1. Tested explicitly in tests/rq1_dlnm/test_data.py.

3.2 Cross-basis (polynomial, not a natural spline)

The design spec originally called for a natural cubic spline (Wood 2006 §5.3). During execution we found the rolled-own ns emitted zero-padded columns for df = 3 (only a linear + one wiggle survive). We replaced it with a standardized degree-3 polynomial:

z = (x − median) / ((max − min)/2),  basis = [z, z², z³]

Knots are only used to compute (center, scale); they are not classical spline knots. In run_sweep.py we set v_knots = (min, median, max) of the observed lag matrix, and lag_knots = (6, 12, 18) over the 0–23 lag range (for a polynomial basis these translate to center = 12, half-range = 6, i.e. z ∈ [−2, +1.83]).

Cross-basis: X[i, v·df_lag + l] = Σₖ B_var(L[i, k])[v] · B_lag(k)[l], contracting over lags. Shape: (n_rows, 3 × 3) = (78, 9).

3.3 Poisson GLM

log μᵢ = α + xᵢᶜᵇ β + γCBSA(i) + δ · yearᵢ + θ · miss_fracᵢ + log nᵢ
  • β ∈ ℝ⁹: cross-basis coefficients, ridge-penalized (λ = 10⁻²).
  • γ: 14 CBSA fixed effects, γ₀ = 0 pinned for identifiability. Unpenalized.
  • δ: linear year trend (year is centered). Unpenalized.
  • θ: imputation-fraction covariate (per-row fraction of missing values in the lag vector). Unpenalized.
  • α: intercept. Unpenalized.

3.4 Fit

PyTorch, Adam, lr = 5 × 10⁻², 2000 steps (bumped from the plan's 500 — see §8), patience = 20 at tol 10⁻⁶. Device: Apple MPS when available, CPU fallback. Full sweep wall time: ~4 minutes on M-series Apple Silicon.

3.5 Inference

Observed-information Hessian on β at the Adam optimum: H = Xᵀ diag(μ̂) X + 2λI. CIs on log-RR via delta method:

c = B(vhigh) − B(vlow),  log RR̂ = cᵀ β̂,  SE = √(cᵀ H⁻¹ c)

Contrast: cumulative log-RR at the 90th vs 10th percentile of the observed lag matrix, relative to the median. Wald z → two-sided p-value. BH-FDR across all 108 pairs, accept at q ≤ 0.1.

7. Itemset pivot (FP-Growth)

7.1 Motivation

The DLNM sweep (§6) produces rankings but not defensible effect sizes. The classical fix is a CBSA-cluster bootstrap, which tightens the SEs but does not change the fundamental limitation: only 98 CBSA-year rows exist, and the outcome panel is yearly. A better move is to ask a question whose inferential assumptions match the data resolution. Association-rule mining does that — it asks "do these items co-occur?", not "what is the conditional expectation curve?". The classical Apriori / FP-Growth toolkit is the natural choice and was, in fact, the checkpoint's originally planned RQ1 method.

7.2 Transaction panel construction

  • Unit of analysis: one CBSA-year per transaction. 14 CBSAs × 7 years = 98 potential transactions; 6 are missing in the merged env-disease panel, giving 92 transactions. (The DLNM panel loses another 14 to the 24-month lookback requirement; the itemset variant does not, so it gets the 14 extra rows back.)
  • Environmental items: for each of the 27 surviving exposures (after the DLNM-side prune), aggregate monthly env to yearly CBSA-year means, then discretize via pd.qcut into quartiles. Each exposure contributes items <col>_Q1 … <col>_Q4. Total: 108 env items.
  • Disease items: for each of the 4 ICD-L1 chapters, flag a CBSA-year as high-prevalence if its prevalence is at or above the chapter's 2/3 quantile across the whole panel. Item names: J00-J99_high, I00-I99_high, F01-F99_high, E00-E89_high. By construction 31/92 ≈ 33.7% of CBSA-years are high in each chapter.
  • Panel shape: 92 × 112 boolean table (env row density is exactly 27 True by construction, disease row density ∈ {0, 1, 2, 3, 4}). 72/92 rows flag at least one chapter high.

7.3 Mining

FP-Growth (mlxtend) with the following thresholds:

min_support = 0.05  ·  min_confidence = 0.5  ·  max_len = 3
  • max_len = 3 is a pragmatic cap. 112 items on 92 rows without a length cap blew up the frequent-itemset enumeration (our first run with no cap and support = 3/92 never returned). With max_len = 3, FP-Growth returns 14,678 frequent itemsets in < 0.1 s.
  • The support floor of 0.05 ≈ 4.6/92 is slightly above the checkpoint notebook's "≥3 supporting transactions" floor and keeps the rule count in the interpretable thousands rather than tens-of-thousands.
  • Association rules are then generated with min_confidence = 0.5 and filtered by the structural requirement: antecedent is a subset of the 108 env items, and consequent contains at least one disease item. This is the key filter — it prevents env⇒env or disease⇒disease "trivial" rules from surviving.

7.5 Reading the rules

The rule lattice divides into a handful of clusters:

  • Cold-soil → endocrine. Both soil_temperature_level_3_Q1 and temperature_2m_Q1 antecedents pull E00-E89_high into the consequent with confidence 0.5-0.6 and lift 3.7-4.0. This is the "cold season, high endocrine prevalence" co-occurrence — the winter-mediated cluster.
  • High vegetation / low latent heat → circulatory. leaf_area_index_high_vegetation_Q4 paired with surface_latent_heat_flux_sum_Q1 predicts I00-I99_high with confidence 0.91 and lift 4.0. Mechanistic reading: this is a seasonal regime (summer/late-spring in Ohio) where LAI is high and latent heat flux is relatively low, and circulatory disease prevalence runs hot. Caveat: the yearly panel collapses seasonality within a year, so "high LAI" here is an annual average — this captures differences between CBSA-years, not within one.
  • Hot-soil → respiratory. soil_temperature_level_3_Q4 + temperature_2m_Q4 → J00-J99_high. Smaller but clearly the "warm-year" cluster for respiratory.
  • Soil-moisture → mental. Low volumetric_soil_water_layer_{1,3} pairs with F01-F99_high. Likely a spurious confounder with regional climate / urbanicity rather than a direct soil-mental link.

9. Deviations from the plan

  1. Basis function. Planned: natural cubic spline (df = 3). Actual: standardized degree-3 polynomial. Trigger: the rolled-own ns emitted zero-padded columns at df = 3. Polynomial preserves df = 3 with stable numerics and required no downstream API change beyond a rename (natural_spline → poly_basis) and a spec §4.1 correction.
  2. Knot locations. Planned: 25th/50th/75th percentiles. Actual: (min, median, max). Trigger: IQR-based standardization extrapolated the z³ term too aggressively at the tails, yielding log-RR values > 1000 in the first sweep. (min, median, max) keeps z in [−1, +1] across the observed range.
  3. Adam steps. Planned: 500. Actual: 2000. Trigger: at 500 steps most fits hit the patience criterion with pathological β (frozen at step-size multiples). At 2000 steps 65/108 converge cleanly.
  4. Device handling. Planned and implemented observed_information reads device from X_cb. The first implementation required a caller-side m.to("cpu"). Post-review, observed_information now reads device from next(model.parameters()) and is caller-safe.
  5. All-NaN rows. impute_and_flag now explicitly handles rows whose entire 24-lag window is NaN (fills 0.0, marks miss_frac = 1.0) instead of emitting a RuntimeWarning. These rows contribute no exposure signal to the fit but carry the miss-fraction covariate.
  6. Bootstrap skipped. Planned as out-of-scope (spec §8); not done. See §9.

10. Known limitations

The big one: inflated significance

101/108 pairs cross BH-FDR q ≤ 0.1. On a 78-row panel with 25 effective parameters, this is not credible — it is overwhelmingly likely that observed-information SEs are underestimating uncertainty. Three contributing causes:

  1. Adam ≠ MLE. Observed-info is only a valid asymptotic covariance at the true MLE. Adam at 2000 steps is close but not exact; 43/108 fits didn't even hit the patience criterion.
  2. Clustering ignored. Each of the 14 CBSAs contributes 6 correlated observations. Fixed effects remove the level, but residual within-CBSA correlation inflates the effective sample size under the independent-observation variance.
  3. Temporal autocorrelation. Year t and year t+1 share 11 months of exposure via the lag matrix. Treating them as independent inflates n by another factor ~2.
Practical consequence. Treat the sweep output as an ordinal ranking — which exposures and which chapters stand out. Do not treat the reported p-values or CI widths as calibrated. The direction of effect and the rank order are usable; the point-estimate magnitudes and nominal significance are not.

Smaller limitations

  • 14 CBSAs. Too few for a spatial random effect. Fixed effects are the ceiling of what the panel can support.
  • Yearly outcomes. The DLNM literature normally runs on daily outcomes. Our sub-annual signal has to be extracted through the cumulative-RR contrast alone; we can't directly see within-year dynamics on the outcome side.
  • No SDI. CBSA-level SDI is not in the SatHealth release. Any socioeconomic confounding is absorbed into the CBSA fixed effects, which is both a strength (we're not pretending to adjust) and a limitation (we can't decompose climate vs. poverty).
  • Correlated exposures. The prune at |r| ≥ 0.98 leaves multiple temperature-family variables that correlate at ~0.9. The sweep treats them as independent tests; a joint fit would be more honest.
  • Marginal, not joint. Each fit has one exposure. Omitted-variable bias is unbounded in principle. For a PoC ranking this is acceptable; for inference on any specific pair it is not.
  • Fit instability across runs. Adam init is not seeded in run_sweep. The top pair changes between runs (lake_bottom_temperature × I00-I99 vs soil_temperature_level_3 × I00-I99 were the #1 pair in two successive runs). Rank order among the top ~8 is stable; exact ordering isn't.

11. Next steps for the final writeup

  1. CBSA-cluster bootstrap for the top DLNM pairs. Replace the asymptotic Wald CI with a resampling CI that respects the 14-CBSA structure. Expected outcome: 101/108 significance rate collapses to something like 20–40/108 and the itemset pivot's conclusion ("treat as ordinal") is quantitatively justified.
  2. Stability of itemset rules. Bootstrap the 92 CBSA-years and re-mine; report rule-retention rate at the top-50-by-lift cut. Noisy support figures on 92 rows make this the single most important sensitivity check for the pivot.
  3. Seed both pipelines. torch.manual_seed in rq1_dlnm.run_sweep.run; the itemset side is deterministic already but document the threshold choices in one place.
  4. Deepen one story. Pick the cold-soil → endocrine cluster (both methods agree, rule has lift 4.0 with confidence 0.52 and DLNM rank 6) and build the lead figure around it: three-panel DLNM surface + a bar chart of itemset rule confidences + a bootstrap CI on the rule's lift.
  5. One joint DLNM fit as a supplement. Ridge-penalized DLNM with temperature + one air-quality + one greenery exposure stacked, to show the marginal ranking is carried by temperature rather than by each variable independently.
  6. Finer-resolution outcome data. SatHealth ships environment at CT and ZCTA levels but outcomes only at CBSA. For a future study, merge county-level CDC WONDER mortality or HCUP SID inpatient data onto the existing SatHealth env panel — that would unlock the classical DLNM design this dataset alone cannot support.
